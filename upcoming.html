Top activities to do in Toronto this week

Canada’s largest city is home to one of the most multicultural populations in the world, which means there is a diverse, ever-evolving set of activities to choose from across all forms of culture. Whether you’re looking to dive into Toronto’s vast number of museums, explore nature in its parks, or sample from its varied cuisines, there is plenty to fill any itinerary. Our recommendation? Take it all in bit by bit. There’s always another chance to come back and see more.

Toronto Music Garden

This urban garden located in Toronto’s Harbourfront neighborhood is very accessible to tourists walking around downtown—and it has great views of the CN Tower. As for the garden itself: Yo-Yo Ma, the cello maestro, can put “landscape architect” on his resume after having collaborated on the Toronto Music Garden with professional landscape architect Julie Moir Messervy. For the project, the duo physically interpreted and manifested Johann Sebastian Bach’s Cello Suite No. 1 in G Major, the first of the composer’s six suites for unaccompanied cello, as a garden, using the piece’s six dance movements (Prelude, Allemande, Courante, Sarabande, Menuet, and Gigue) to create different sections of the green space. At the center is the Gigue (or Jig) section, a grassy amphitheater with a stage beneath a weeping willow tree where free classical music concerts are held each summer.

Art Gallery of Ontario

Both the permanent and temporary exhibits are well-thought out and well-presented, and the topics of world-class special shows are as varied and detailed as the regular collection

Zoom out. What’s this place all about?

From the works of European masters, Central African artists, and emerging Indigenous Canadians to cutting-edge contemporary pieces that challenge and inspire, you’ll find it all in this museum’s nearly-100,000-piece-strong collection. In addition to being one of the largest art museums in North America, it’s also one of Canada’s most photographed architectural sites—thanks to the soaring 2008 expansion designed by Frank Gehry.

Ye shall know them by their permanent collection: How was it?

There's a lot here to marvel at (and process), so plan to settle in for a chunk of time—or come back again. Or zero in on your passions. Into photography? Revel in a collection that includes pieces by Garry Winogrand and Diane Arbus. Fine art fans will be transported by the landscapes of the legendary Group of Seven Canadian painters, the rich tones of Rubens’ The Massacre of The Innocents, and the details of the Gothic miniatures. And thanks to special exhibits that highlight emerging and underrepresented artists, there’s always something new to discover.

But also by their new shoes. How were the exhibits?

Both the permanent and temporary exhibits are well-thought-out and well-presented, and the topics of world-class special shows are as varied and detailed as the regular collection. For example, past visiting shows have spotlighted the Ojibwe community and language, the “monsters” in the works of filmmaker Guillermo del Toro, and Yayoi Kusama’s crowd-pleasing Infinity Mirrors light installation.
